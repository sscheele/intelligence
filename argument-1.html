Consciousness

The essence of information

  • Assumptions

Consciousness. We all know what it’s like. As you’re reading this, you’re experiencing it. However, philosophically speaking, we can’t really tell what it is; we can’t quantify it in any way, shape, or form. So how do we determine if something is conscious?

The answer is actually quite simple. It really isn’t much of a stretch for a human to assume that other humans are conscious-- even given that all other humans may be “philosophical zombies”. The logical extension of this when applied to artificial intelligence gives us a handy tool for defining consciousness: The Turing Test. If a machine passes the test by exhibiting behavior that is indistinguishable from a human, then it should be considered human. However, we’re not just here to define consciousness in terms of humanity. Instead, we should assume that if something appears conscious, then it should be treated as such.

However, we’re not here to simply extend an old, possibly outdated tool to other forms of intelligence. We’re here to completely redefine philosophy. Thankfully, information philosopher Luciano Floridi has laid out the groundwork for us. He and other information philosophers propose that “the fundamental metaphysical connection between materialism and idealism” is information (Information Philosopher). Basically, material things and ideas are connected by information. What this means for the individual is that information is the link between the body and the mind. Because of this, information is the way through which conscious beings interface with reality. This allows us to define conscious beings as those which have the capacity to generate new information. That’s not to say that an algorithm that takes inputs and turn out outputs is conscious, however. New in this sense means original. In a sense, consciousness requires imagination.
