Every branch of ethics assigns intrinsic value to some idea. In deontology it’s duty; in utilitarianism it’s utility; virtue theory it’s virtue. Accordingly to value theory, moral activity maximizes value. In information ethics, the highest value is, unsurprisingly, information. Accordingly to information philosophy, entropy is the enemy of information, as chaotic states of matter reduce the amount of information available for use. Information, locally, works against the Second Law of Thermodynamics as a kind of negative entropy.  For the purposes of this argument, we won’t go into this here. However, you can find out more here: http://www.informationphilosopher.com/problems/reversibility/ .

In his Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals , Immanuel Kant describes that in order for people to be moral they must act by a categorical imperative. The requisites for the categorical imperative are as follows:

  1. Act only according to that maxim by which you can also will that it would become a universal law.
  2. Act in such a way that you always treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never simply as a means, but always at the same time as an end.
  3. Every rational being must so act as if he were through his maxim always a legislating member in a universal kingdom of ends.

The universality of ethics described by the categorical imperative are reflected in information ethics. In a sense, the categorical imperative in information ethics is to act in such a way that maximizes information and reduces entropy. This, along with the definition of consciousness that information philosophy provides, will lay the groundwork for our arguments about the treatment of non-human intelligences.
